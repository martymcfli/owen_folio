The Retention Engine: Why CX is Your Best Growth Strategy

By Owen McCormick 5 min read

I built a $2.5M business by walking into stores cold — no connections, no marketing budget, just showing up and being straight with people. That experience shaped everything I believe about how businesses actually grow.

The honest truth I kept running into: most companies are so focused on finding new customers that they completely neglect the ones they already have. And that's backwards. The hardest part is getting someone to trust you the first time. Once they do, keeping that trust costs almost nothing compared to starting over with a stranger.

Something I've seen over and over

"The companies that treat customer experience as an afterthought spend a fortune chasing new business. The ones that get it right barely have to."

People stay when they feel like you actually care

When I built the ordering system at Trail Blazers, I wasn't thinking about throughput or operational metrics. I was thinking about the store owner who had to wait three days to find out if their order went through. That's the thing that was eating at them. Fix that, and you've done something real.

Customers don't leave because your product is slightly worse than the competition. They leave because they felt like a number. They leave because nobody followed up, or because getting help was a hassle, or because something small went wrong and nobody owned it. When you build systems around actually serving people — not just processing them — they stick around.

The best referral you'll ever get is free

I never ran a single ad for Trail Blazers. Growth came entirely from store owners telling other store owners. That only happens when someone genuinely likes doing business with you — not when they're just satisfied, but when the experience was good enough that it came up in conversation.

Whether it was sorting out a warranty dispute for a Porsche client or making sure athlete check-in ran smoothly at an Olympic qualifier, I've always cared about whether someone walked away feeling like it went well. Not just "was it resolved" but "did they feel like we had their back." That's what people talk about. Your reputation is built in those moments, not in your marketing copy.

What I actually do about it

When I look at a customer-facing operation, I'm looking for three things:

  • 1 Where are people getting stuck? Every place a customer has to wait, guess, or follow up is a place they're quietly losing confidence in you. Find those spots and fix them.
  • 2 Can the person closest to the problem actually solve it? If your frontline team has to escalate everything, you're making customers wait for no reason and burning out good people in the process.
  • 3 Are you measuring what actually matters? Fast response times are great. But did the customer feel like you listened? Those aren't always the same thing.

None of this is complicated in theory. It just takes being honest about where the gaps are and being willing to do something about them — even when it means changing how things have always been done.
