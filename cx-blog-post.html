The Retention Engine: Why CX is Your Best Growth Strategy

By Owen McCormick 5 min read

I spent a few years building a business by going door to door to stores with nothing but a product and a handshake. No ads, no connections. Just showing up and doing right by people.

What I noticed pretty quickly is that the stores who kept ordering from me weren't just happy with the product. They trusted me. I picked up the phone when they called, sorted out problems without making them chase me, and remembered the stuff they told me. That was pretty much it.

Something I keep coming back to

The companies that pour everything into finding new customers and ignore the ones they have usually struggle a lot more than they need to.

It mostly comes down to trust

People don't usually leave because they found something better. They leave because they got ignored, or a problem dragged on too long, or they just stopped feeling like anyone cared. When I built out the ordering system at Trail Blazers, I wasn't trying to optimize a process. I was trying to make sure store owners weren't left wondering what happened to their order for three days. Small thing, but it mattered a lot to them.

When you set things up with the customer in mind, people stick around. And when they stick around, everything else gets easier.

Happy customers tell people

Trail Blazers grew almost entirely through word of mouth. I didn't have a marketing budget so I didn't have much choice, but honestly it worked out better than any ad would have. When someone tells a friend about you, that actually means something.

At Porsche it was the same idea, just a different setting. Whether I was sorting out a service dispute or just making sure someone felt like their concern was heard, the goal was always to have them walk away glad they reached out. That's the stuff people remember and talk about.

What I look for when I come into a role

Three pretty simple things:

  • 1 Where are people getting frustrated and why? It's usually obvious once you start paying attention to it.
  • 2 Does the person handling the problem actually have the ability to fix it? If not, customers end up going in circles and that's a process problem, not a people problem.
  • 3 Are we measuring the right things? Fast response times are good, but they don't tell you if the person felt like they were actually helped.

None of this is complicated. It just takes being willing to look honestly at what's not working and actually do something about it.
